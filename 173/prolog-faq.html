Only accounting for "all" and "some" is completely fine. For extra credit, you could do "any" (for all), "a" (there exists), "an" (there exists), and "the" (there exists).

Why isn't "The boy who the girl likes likes a watermelon" grammatically correct?
Although the boy is the subject of the sentence, the boy is also the direct object of "the girl likes". Thus, it should be "The boy whom the girl likes likes a watermelon".

Is there a way to implement "OR" in Prolog across different statements -- just have a query return "True" and finish if at least one possibility is "True"?
Yes, you can surround queries with once, or to be more elegant, you can try putting a cut (!) right before the end of each definition.

No, reading and translating sentences with past tenses is optional (bonus).

What is the best way to make sure my program thinks the sentence "The boy who the girl likes likes a watermelon" is ungrammatical? Should I include an extra argument in order to state whether a noun is a direct object or not?
I believe you can solve that problem by looking at the form of the relative clause. For example, the rule "RELCL --> Rel VP" implies that Rel refers to a subject, such as in "The boy who likes a watermelon likes an apple." On the other hand, the rule "RELCL--> NP V" implies that Rel refers to a direct object, such as in "The boy whom the girl likes likes a watermelon".

How would we translate a sentence like "Some boys eat apples" to FOL given that there is no quantifier in front of "apples"?
By convention, if there is no quantifier in front of a noun, it means "for all". Thus, the sentence could be rephrased as "some boys eat all apples".

In our parse trees, are we supposed to leave the plural form of a noun in the tree, or always output the singular version?
Either one is fine. For example, if your sentence was "some boys eat some apple", I will accept either sentence(noun_phrase(determiner(some), noun(boy)), verb_phrase(verb(eat), noun_phrase(determiner(some), noun(apple)))), or sentence(noun_phrase(determiner(some), noun(boys)), verb_phrase(verb(eat), noun_phrase(determiner(some), noun(apple)))).
